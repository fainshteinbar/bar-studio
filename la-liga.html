The Problem

A Global Giant
With a Local
Blindspot.

La Liga is one of the most recognized football brands on earth — Real Madrid, Barcelona, El Clásico. But in Israel, the official licensed entity was operating like a generic social media account. Global brand equity was enormous. Local relevance was near zero.

Israeli fans watch La Liga obsessively — but they had no reason to follow the official Hebrew channel. The content didn't speak their language or reflect local football culture. It felt copied from the global feed, not built for them. Every post was broadcasting to a community that never asked to be spoken to in someone else's voice.

I was brought in to change that. My mandate: build a localized brand system that made La Liga feel genuinely Israeli — without diluting the global equity. Beyond the Big Two: I insisted every campaign spotlight the smaller clubs — Girona, Sevilla, Rayo — creating content that educated the local audience on the full league, not just the marquee names.

The Brief I Accepted

"Connect the world's most glamorous league to a fan base that speaks a different language, carries different cultural references, and has never been spoken to directly."

— What I built the entire system around.

My Process

How I Built
the Bridge

01

Speaking Hebrew

I made every post feel native — not translated. The Hebrew copy I directed wasn't a localization of the global feed. It used local slang, referenced Israeli broadcast culture, and adopted the emotional register of how Israeli football fans actually talk. When fans recognized their own voice coming from an official channel, they stopped scrolling. The engagement data confirmed it immediately.

02

Beyond the Big Two

I pushed back on the instinct to only post Real Madrid and Barcelona. Israeli fans already follow those clubs across a dozen global accounts. The differentiator was the rest of the league — Girona's historic Champions League run, Rayo Vallecano's working-class identity, Sevilla's cup pedigree. I built photomontages and interactive graphics featuring players the local audience had never seen profiled before. That novelty created loyalty.

03

Interactive Content System

I designed a content architecture that turned passive followers into active participants. Polls on lineup decisions. Bracket challenges. "What would you do?" scenario posts timed to live match moments. I integrated this interactive layer with La Liga's global content calendar without duplicating it — the local posts added value rather than echoing what fans could already get from the main account.

04

Visual Excellence at Scale

I designed a modular visual system that allowed high-quality, on-brand content to be produced consistently across the full matchday cycle — pre-match, live, post-match, weekly features — without losing coherence or requiring art direction on every frame. The template architecture I built let the operation scale output significantly while I maintained creative oversight at the brief and review level.

What I Learned

What This
Project
Proved.

A 2,305% follower increase isn't a metric. It's evidence of a system working. Every percentage point traces back to a specific creative decision — and knowing which decision drove which result is what separates a creative director from a content producer.

Localization Is Architecture, Not Translation

The most important decision I made wasn't about color or typography. It was building a structural framework that defined exactly which brand elements were inviolable and which could flex for local expression. That architecture protected the global asset while enabling the local authenticity that drove the numbers.

The Audience Tells You What Works

Knowing that the small-club profiles outperformed the Big Two content wasn't a prediction — it was a discovery. I built the system to surface those signals fast and redirect creative energy accordingly. That's not intuition. That's infrastructure.

Template Systems Are Multipliers

Producing 1,000+ pieces of content per month at consistent quality isn't possible through effort alone. The modular visual system I designed was the leverage — it made every team member more capable and kept my creative direction embedded in every piece without requiring my hands on every frame.
