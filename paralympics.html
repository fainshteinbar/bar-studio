The Brief

A New Logo
for a New
Chapter.

The Israel Paralympic Committee needed to rebuild its brand from the ground up. A new logo. A complete visual architecture. An identity that could represent Israeli Paralympic sport at the highest international level — from Tokyo to Paris to Los Angeles.

Paralympic identity work sits at the intersection of national pride, athletic excellence, and inclusivity — a combination that's easy to get wrong. Too nationalistic and it ignores the global Paralympic movement. Too generic and it fails to represent Israel specifically. Too soft and it misrepresents what Paralympic sport actually is: the most demanding athletic competition in the world.

I designed the new identity by starting at the intersection of those tensions, not trying to avoid them. The result is a brand that carries national weight, communicates athletic intensity, and belongs unmistakably to the Paralympic family — without looking like a copy of any existing Paralympic brand.

The Standard

"Paralympic athletes compete at the limits of human performance. The brand I designed had to be as serious as they are."

— The principle behind every design decision.

The Paralympic movement's Agitos symbol — three asymmetric shapes in red, blue, and green — is one of the most internationally recognized marks in sport. Any national committee brand must co-exist with it without competing or imitating. I designed the Israeli identity to be a distinct system that works alongside the global symbol, not a variation of it. The new logo for Israel's committee reads as clearly Israeli and clearly Paralympic without borrowing visual logic from either the Agitos or the Israeli national crest.

What I Built

Ground-Up
Brand Architecture

01

Logo Design

I designed a new logo for the Israel Paralympic Committee that could function as the visual cornerstone of the entire national Paralympic brand — from athlete uniforms to broadcast graphics to official communications. The mark I created works in Hebrew and Latin, scales from digital favicon to physical medal, and carries enough visual authority to represent Israel at IPC-sanctioned events internationally.

02

Color & Visual Language

I built a color system that balanced national identity (the Israeli blue and white as a foundation) with the energy and dynamism required for a high-performance sport brand. The expanded palette I designed introduced accent colors that gave the system range — from official committee communications to athlete celebration content — without losing coherence or national identity.

03

Brand Architecture

The IPC Israel covers multiple sports disciplines, from wheelchair basketball to athletics to swimming. I designed a parent-child architecture that allowed each sport to have visual expression appropriate to its discipline while remaining unmistakably part of the national Paralympic brand. The system also needed to work alongside IPC global assets — I built in compatibility from the start rather than treating it as a retrofit.

04

Campaign Direction

I directed the campaigns that launched the new identity — internally to athletes and federation partners, and publicly across digital and out-of-home. The Paralympic Games cycle (Tokyo 2020 → Paris 2024) gave the work two major public moments. I designed both rollouts to be appropriate to their context: the Tokyo announcement was necessarily digital-first; Paris allowed for a broader physical presence. Both campaigns centered the athletes, not the committee.

What I Learned

National
Identity Is
Complex Work.

Designing for a national governing body means your work represents a country, not just an organization. Every design decision carries that weight. This project taught me what it means to design at that scale of responsibility.

International Standards Require International Research

Before designing a single mark, I studied how every major Paralympic committee brand operated — how they balanced national and global identity, which visual strategies held up across games cycles, what distinguished the strongest national brands from the forgettable ones. That research shaped every decision I made for Israel's committee.

Durability Is a Design Requirement

A national committee brand isn't redesigned every season. It needs to work across four-year Olympic cycles, leadership changes, new sport disciplines, and shifting cultural contexts. I designed the IPC Israel identity to be durable first — which meant being conservative about trend-driven choices and generous with systematic flexibility.

Athletes Are the Clients You Design For

The Israel Paralympic Committee's stakeholders include board members, federation partners, sponsors, and government liaisons. But the real clients — the people the brand ultimately serves — are the athletes. Every decision I made was filtered through the question: does this represent them with the seriousness they deserve?
