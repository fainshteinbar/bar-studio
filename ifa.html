The Challenge

A Legacy Brand
Frozen in
the Past.

The Israel Football Association is one of the country's oldest sports institutions — a FIFA and UEFA member with decades of heritage, infrastructure, and authority. But its visual identity hadn't kept pace. The brand looked like a government department when it needed to feel like a federation.

Federations operate at a different register than clubs. They don't sell tickets — they sell the game itself. The visual language must carry the weight of the whole sport: from youth football to the national team, from women's competitions to referee programs. One brand. Every touchpoint. No inconsistency.

My job was to architect a brand system that could hold all of that — modern enough for the digital era, authoritative enough to represent a FIFA member, and flexible enough to govern every sub-competition, division, and program under the IFA umbrella.

The Standard I Held

"A national football federation's brand must sit on the same stage as UEFA and FIFA visual communication — and not look out of place."

— The benchmark I designed to.

UEFA
Full UEFA member. Every visual asset benchmarked against UEFA's own brand standards — from color authority to typographic weight to motion language. Federation brands that look cheap next to the governing body undermine institutional credibility.
FIFA
World Cup qualifiers, international friendlies, officiating materials — all required absolute alignment with FIFA's identity framework. I built the IFA system to be FIFA-compatible from day one, so joint communications never required renegotiating design decisions.
IFA
The domestic identity had to be distinctly Israeli — not a neutral "member federation" template. I embedded cultural specificity into the system: Hebrew-first typography, national colors with institutional authority, tone that balanced official gravitas with genuine football energy.

What I Built

The Federation
Brand System

01

Brand Architecture Audit

Before designing anything new, I mapped the full ecosystem: every sub-brand, competition, and program under the IFA umbrella. The State Cup. The Premier League. Women's football. Youth academies. Referee development. Each had informal visual approaches that had evolved independently — creating an inconsistent patchwork rather than a system. I documented the full landscape before deciding what to keep, what to standardize, and what to rebuild.

02

Identity Modernization

I redesigned the IFA's core visual identity to read as a contemporary federation — not a sports team or a government agency. That meant rethinking typographic hierarchy (Hebrew-first display type, Latin secondary), the color system (national colors with authority-grade neutrals and gradients), and the overall graphic language. I built the identity to be scalable: from favicon to stadium-sized banner without quality degradation.

03

Sub-Brand Governance

The IFA's complexity required a parent-child brand architecture. I designed a governance framework that gave each competition and program its own visual expression while remaining unmistakably IFA. The system defined exactly how far sub-brands could deviate from the parent identity, which design elements were non-negotiable, and how the hierarchy reads across different contexts and audiences.

04

Digital & Social Architecture

I built a modular content system that allowed the IFA's multiple channels — national team, domestic competitions, women's football, grassroots — to produce consistent, on-brand content without centralized art direction on every post. Template hierarchies, color-coded by competition type, Hebrew/Latin bilingual layout systems: any content creator could produce federation-standard work within the guardrails I built.

05

Campaign & Motion Direction

I directed the motion identity for high-stakes federation moments — World Cup qualifier announcements, national team kit reveals, competition launches. The motion language I designed referenced European federation communication standards: authoritative pacing, dramatic typography reveals, controlled use of the national palette. Every animation was built to work as a standalone asset and as part of a coordinated rollout across broadcast, digital, and out-of-home.

What I Learned

Federation
Work Is
Different.

Working with a national federation taught me that brand decisions at the institutional level carry consequences that ripple further and last longer than campaign work. You're not building for a season. You're building for a generation.

Systems Govern Better Than Rules

A brand guideline document is a list of rules. A brand system is a set of decisions already made, embedded into templates and tools that make good work the path of least resistance. I built the IFA's identity as a system — not a style guide — and the consistency across channels proved the difference.

Authority Comes From Restraint

The temptation in federation work is to make everything look important. But institutional authority communicates through restraint — controlled color palettes, confident negative space, typography that doesn't shout. The IFA's new identity looked more powerful with fewer elements, not more.

Internal Alignment Is Half the Work

A brand system only works if the people inside the organization understand and believe in it. At the IFA, I spent as much time presenting and building internal buy-in as designing. Getting multiple departments — national team, competitions, comms — to adopt a unified visual language required creative leadership beyond the Figma file.
