The Brief

Build an Identity
From Zero.
Make It Feel Human.

Special Olympics Israel had a unique challenge: they needed a visual identity that was distinctly Israeli — not a licensed copy of the global Special Olympics brand applied with a Hebrew logo. The Israeli chapter has its own events, its own athletes, its own emotional story. The brand needed to reflect that individuality.

The global Special Olympics brand is warm, inclusive, and celebratory — but it's designed to be the same everywhere. My brief was different: create an identity for the Israeli chapter that could stand on its own, honor the global family it belongs to, and still communicate something specific about these athletes, in this country, in this cultural context.

I started with the athletes themselves. What does it feel like to compete in the Special Olympics? What does this movement mean in Israel specifically? The answers to those questions became the design brief. I built the logo, color system, typography, and content architecture entirely from those insights — not from a template.

The Principle Behind the Work

"Every athlete in the Special Olympics is competing at the limit of what they can do. The brand had to feel like that energy — not like a charity poster."

— The creative direction I built the system around.

What I Designed

The Full
Identity System

01

Original Logo Design

I designed the logo from scratch — not adapted from the global Special Olympics mark, not templated from another branch. I researched what made the Israeli chapter distinct: the specific sports featured, the cultural relationship to disability and sport in Israeli society, the community that had grown around the events. The final mark communicates motion, aspiration, and community — in a visual language that is distinctly Israeli without being kitschy about it.

02

Color System

I built a color palette that felt energetic and celebratory without defaulting to the generic "inclusive sports" visual language of pastels and rainbows. The system is bold — warm orange as the dominant accent, deep purple for depth and gravity, sky blue for openness and aspiration. The combination communicates the full emotional range of the Special Olympics experience: joy, effort, and triumph, not charity.

03

Emotional Campaign Direction

I directed campaigns that centered on the athletes as competitors, not subjects of sympathy. Every creative decision pushed toward dignity: close-up photography that showed effort and expression rather than disability, copy that used active language about achievement rather than passive language about participation. The campaigns I directed positioned Special Olympics Israel as a sports organization that happens to include athletes with intellectual disabilities — not the other way around.

04

Content Architecture

I built a social content system that could handle the full calendar: seasonal events, athlete profiles, competition coverage, sponsor integrations, awareness campaigns. The system was designed for a lean team — intuitive templates, clear rules for what content goes where, and an editorial voice that matched the brand's emotional direction without requiring a senior creative on every post.

What I Learned

Design for
Athletes, Not
Audiences.

The most important design decision on this project wasn't about aesthetics — it was about perspective. I built the identity from the athlete's point of view, not the spectator's. That shift changed everything about the visual language.

Emotional Branding Requires Courage

The easy choice was the charity aesthetic — soft colors, inspirational quotes, stock photography of smiling faces. The harder and more important choice was to design a brand that looked like sport. That choice required conviction in every client conversation, not just the design decisions.

A Branch Can Be Distinct From Its Global Parent

The global Special Olympics brand is powerful, but it's designed to be generic enough for 200 countries. Designing an original identity for the Israeli chapter — one that was distinctly local without breaking from the family — proved that national chapters don't have to accept visual anonymity in exchange for belonging to a global movement.

Identity Design Is Research First

The logo I designed for Special Olympics Israel would not exist without the time I spent understanding the athletes, the organization's culture, and what made the Israeli chapter distinct. The best creative decisions on this project were made before I opened a design file. Research isn't pre-work. It's the work.
